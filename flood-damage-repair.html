What is Flood Damage Repair?

Flood damage repair is the specialized process of restoring properties after flooding from external water sources. Unlike typical water damage from internal plumbing, flood water brings unique challenges including contamination, sediment, structural damage, and higher volumes of water. In Reno, flooding primarily occurs from Truckee River overflow during heavy snowmelt, severe thunderstorms that overwhelm drainage systems, and occasional sewage backups when municipal systems get overloaded.

Flood damage is almost always Category 3 (black water), meaning it contains dangerous contaminants like bacteria, sewage, chemicals, and debris. This requires specialized safety protocols, protective equipment, and disposal procedures. Our technicians are trained in hazmat handling and follow strict EPA and OSHA guidelines for flood restoration.

Flood Risks in Reno and Washoe County

Truckee River Flooding

The Truckee River runs through downtown Reno and poses flood risks during years with heavy Sierra snowpack followed by rapid spring warming. The 1997 New Year's flood remains the most devastating in Reno's recent history, but smaller floods occurred in 2005, 2011, and 2017. Properties near the river in areas like Idlewild Park, West Street, and parts of downtown face the highest risk.

When the Truckee floods, water rises quickly and brings debris, sediment, and contamination. Properties in the 100-year flood plain require separate flood insurance (homeowners policies don't cover flood damage). If you're in a flood zone and experience flooding, you need specialists who understand how to handle contaminated water and meet FEMA requirements for restoration.

Flash Flooding from Summer Storms

Reno's high desert location makes it vulnerable to intense summer thunderstorms that dump rain faster than drainage systems can handle. Areas at the bottom of slopes or near washes can experience flash flooding even when total rainfall is modest. Neighborhoods in South Reno near foothills and areas with poor drainage are most vulnerable.

Flash flood water picks up oil, debris, and contaminants from streets and carries them into homes. Basements and ground-floor rooms flood first. This type of flooding happens fast and often catches homeowners off guard. Having our emergency number (775) 555-0142 saved in your phone means you can call immediately when water starts entering your home.

Sewer Backups

When Reno's sewer systems get overloaded during storms or experience blockages, sewage can back up into homes through floor drains, toilets, and lower plumbing fixtures. This is the worst type of flood damage because sewage contains dangerous bacteria, viruses, and parasites. Sewer backups require immediate professional response, complete material removal, and extensive sanitization.

Many homeowners don't realize that standard homeowners insurance often doesn't cover sewer backups unless you purchased a specific sewer backup rider. We can help you document the damage properly and work with your insurance to maximize coverage. Even if insurance doesn't cover it, we offer financing options because sewer backup cleanup cannot wait.

Our Flood Damage Restoration Process

Step 1: Safety Assessment and Securing the Property

When we arrive at a flood-damaged property, safety comes first. We assess structural stability, check for electrical hazards, identify contamination levels, and determine if the property is safe to enter. Our team wears full protective equipment including waterproof suits, boots, gloves, and respirators when dealing with contaminated flood water. We'll turn off electricity to affected areas and secure the property to prevent further damage or unauthorized entry.

Step 2: Water Extraction and Debris Removal

Flood water often contains mud, debris, and sediment that must be removed before drying can begin. We use truck-mounted extractors to remove standing water, then manually remove debris and sediment. Everything that touched contaminated flood water gets documented for insurance and properly disposed of according to EPA regulations. This phase is labor-intensive and can take 1-3 days depending on the volume of water and debris.

Step 3: Demolition and Material Removal

Most porous materials that contacted Category 3 flood water cannot be saved and must be removed. This typically includes drywall up to 12-24 inches above the water line, carpeting and padding, insulation, baseboards, and sometimes subflooring. We document everything with photos before removal for insurance purposes. All contaminated materials get bagged and disposed of at approved facilities.

Step 4: Antimicrobial Treatment and Sanitization

After demolition, we apply EPA-registered antimicrobial treatments to all surfaces that contacted flood water. This kills bacteria, viruses, and mold spores. We treat wall cavities, concrete floors, framing lumber, and any materials that will remain in place. This step is crucial for preventing future health hazards and is required by most insurance companies for flood claims.

Step 5: Structural Drying

Once the property is clean and sanitized, we begin the drying process. We set up commercial dehumidifiers and air movers to dry framing, concrete, and other structural elements. Flood-damaged properties often require 5-10 days of drying because water penetrates deep into materials. We monitor daily with moisture meters and thermal imaging to ensure complete drying before reconstruction begins.

Step 6: Reconstruction

The final phase involves rebuilding everything that was removed. We install new drywall, insulation, flooring, baseboards, and paint. If FEMA or local building codes require upgrades (like elevated electrical outlets in flood zones), we handle those modifications. Our goal is to restore your property to pre-flood condition or better while meeting all current building codes.

Flood Damage vs. Water Damage: What's the Difference?

Many people use these terms interchangeably, but there are important differences:

  • Water damage comes from internal sources like burst pipes, leaking appliances, or roof leaks. The water is usually Category 1 (clean) or Category 2 (gray). Standard homeowners insurance covers this.
  • Flood damage comes from external water sources like river overflow, heavy rain runoff, or storm surge. The water is almost always Category 3 (black water) with serious contamination. Standard homeowners insurance does NOT cover this; you need separate flood insurance.

For restoration purposes, flood damage requires more extensive material removal, stronger antimicrobial treatments, longer drying times, and stricter safety protocols. It's also typically more expensive. However, many properties in Reno experience what looks like flooding but is actually covered under homeowners insurance if the water source was internal (like a sewer backup from a blocked pipe on your property).

We help you determine the water source and work with your insurance company to maximize coverage. Sometimes what seems like "flooding" is actually covered water damage, and we know how to document it properly.

Does Insurance Cover Flood Damage in Reno?

This is one of the most confusing aspects of flood damage. Here's what you need to know:

Standard homeowners insurance does NOT cover flooding from external sources. If the Truckee River floods your home or heavy rain causes water to enter from outside, your regular homeowners policy won't cover it. You need separate flood insurance through FEMA's National Flood Insurance Program or a private flood policy.

Homeowners insurance MAY cover water damage that looks like flooding. If you have a sewer backup that originated from a blockage on your property (not the city main), it may be covered. If heavy rain causes a roof leak that floods your home from inside, that's covered. If your sump pump fails and your basement floods, that might be covered depending on your policy.

We help you navigate these gray areas. We document the water source, take detailed photos, and provide reports that clearly explain what happened. We've successfully helped hundreds of Reno homeowners get claims approved that initially seemed questionable.

If you're in a flood zone and don't have flood insurance, you may qualify for FEMA disaster assistance if the flooding was part of a declared disaster. We can help you apply and provide the documentation FEMA requires.

Health Hazards from Flood Water

Flood water is dangerous and should never be treated casually. It contains numerous health hazards:

  • Bacteria and viruses: E. coli, Salmonella, Hepatitis, and other pathogens thrive in flood water. Contact with contaminated water can cause serious illness.
  • Sewage contaminants: When sewers overflow or back up, flood water contains raw sewage with extreme pathogen levels.
  • Chemical contamination: Flood water picks up gasoline, oil, pesticides, cleaning chemicals, and industrial waste as it flows through urban areas.
  • Mold growth: Flood-damaged buildings develop mold within 24-48 hours. Some mold species produce dangerous mycotoxins.
  • Sharp debris: Flood water often contains broken glass, nails, sharp metal, and other hazards hidden under murky water.

This is why you should never attempt DIY flood cleanup. Our technicians wear protective equipment, follow strict safety protocols, and have training in hazmat handling. We also carry the insurance and licensing required for handling contaminated materials. Attempting flood cleanup yourself puts you at serious health risk and can lead to long-term problems like toxic mold growth.

Preventing Flood Damage to Your Reno Home

While we can't control when the Truckee River floods or when severe storms hit, you can reduce flood risk:

  • Know your flood zone. Check FEMA flood maps at FloodSmart.gov to see if your property is in a high-risk area. If yes, buy flood insurance (there's a 30-day waiting period before coverage starts).
  • Install a sump pump. If you have a basement or below-grade areas, a quality sump pump with battery backup can prevent flooding from groundwater or minor intrusion.
  • Improve drainage around your home. Make sure ground slopes away from your foundation. Keep gutters clean. Extend downspouts at least 6 feet from your foundation.
  • Install a backwater valve. This prevents sewage from backing up into your home during system overloads. Costs $1,000-$2,000 but can save you $10,000+ in cleanup costs.
  • Create a flood emergency kit. Keep important documents in waterproof containers on upper floors. Have a plan for quick evacuation if flooding becomes severe.
  • Know where your main water shutoff is. If flooding occurs, shutting off your water prevents additional damage from your own plumbing.

What to Do When Flooding Occurs

If your Reno home experiences flooding:

  1. Evacuate if water is rising rapidly. Your safety is more important than property. Move to upper floors or leave entirely if flooding is severe.
